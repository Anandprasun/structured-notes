The skill's value is not a dramatic quality jump on any single note; it is consistency and guardrails: it never invented a fact, never fabricated a decisions section, and preserved author voice — the exact places where the baseline occasionally slipped. Testing also found and fixed one real weakness (redundancy), which is what pushed it ahead.

Fidelity — the highest-stakes dimension

With skill: 0 issues across all 20 cases. Every dosage, dollar figure, date, and verbatim quote was preserved exactly — including furosemide 40mg BID, INR 4.1, $4.6M vs $4.9M, and the witness quote "make the numbers work or we're both finished."
Baseline: 2 real slips. Case 08 — invented a month ("renewal auto-charge Jan 20" when the input only said "the 20th"). Case 12 (legal intake) — introduced a derived total "Damages so far ~$10.2K" not stated in the source. Both are the kind of small, plausible fabrication that erodes trust in high-stakes notes — and exactly what the skill's "never invent" rule prevents.

What we changed from testing

Fix: "Say each thing once"

The one consistent complaint was redundancy — meetings restated a decision in both the Discussion gist and the Decisions section; brain-dumps echoed Ideas as Open loops; reading notes repeated reactions as follow-ups. Added a rule making the pulled-out sections (Decisions, Actions) the single home for outcomes, with the body giving reasoning/context rather than restating. Re-ran the 4 affected cases: all went 15→16, conciseness 1→2, with no loss of facts and no regressions.

How to keep testing this (repeatable plan)

  • The suite is reusable. 20 graded inputs live in eval-suite/inputs/; re-run any time you change the skill and compare to benchmark.json.
  • Watch conciseness and fidelity — those are the two dimensions where note-formatters actually differ; the rest saturate at "good".
  • Add adversarial cases as you find real-world misses: inputs with contradictory statements, half-finished sentences, mixed languages, numbers that look like dates, or a "decision" that was actually just a suggestion.
  • Grade blind and at finer granularity. A 0–2 scale saturates; a 0–4 scale or blind A/B (skill vs baseline, judge doesn't know which) will separate near-ties more sharply.
  • Test triggering separately — whether the skill fires on the right prompts is a different question from output quality; the skill-creator has a description-optimizer for that.

Rubric: type detection · glanceability · structure · action-items · decisions · fidelity · conciseness · voice. Each 0–2, graded by independent rubric-driven reviewers reading input + both outputs. Full per-case JSON in eval-suite/grading/.
